## [atlassian-esg] Built Atlassian's ESG volunteer programs into a repeatable engagement engine

tags: ops, program, stakeholder-coordination, esg, mission | hero: true
company: Atlassian | role: Program & Operations Lead (Renewals) | timeframe: Aug 2021 – Apr 2026

context: Atlassian, enterprise SaaS. I held renewal operations for a large customer portfolio while leading the team's ESG and volunteer programs as my lead contribution — organizing outings, employee-engagement events, and nonprofit partnerships alongside the day job.
problem: ESG and volunteer initiatives were ad-hoc — run opportunistically, with no consistent cadence, weak cross-team visibility, and uneven employee participation. Engagement spiked around individual events then faded.
approach: Treated the programs like an operations pipeline: recurring cadence, named owners per initiative, and a lightweight tracking loop so engagement carried between events instead of restarting from zero each quarter.
decision: Own the program coordination end-to-end rather than hand it to a committee — traded broader buy-in for speed and accountability. The trade-off was personal bandwidth; the win was a single throat-to-strangle and consistent execution.
solution: Standing volunteer-program calendar, partner relationships with the nonprofits we returned to, and an engagement follow-through loop (participation tracked, blockers surfaced, retros after each cycle).

reflection: I ran this on operational instinct, not a measurement framework from day one. In hindsight I'd instrument participation and partnership durability from the start so the impact is provable, not asserted — exactly the gap a deck like this forces into the open.

## [renewals-coordination] Cut renewal coordination cycle time by two weeks across a $50M portfolio

tags: ops, stakeholder-coordination, account-operations, escalations | hero: true
company: Atlassian | role: Program & Operations Lead (Renewals) / Lead Renewal Specialist (5 quarters) | timeframe: Aug 2021 – Apr 2026

context: Owned lifecycle operations for a large customer portfolio (~$50M), coordinating renewals across CS, sales, finance, legal, procurement, channel, and deal support. Served as Lead Renewal Specialist for five quarters — mentoring, escalations, and manager-level coverage — sustaining a 95% team success rate.
problem: Cross-functional renewal cycles were slow and risky — timelines slipped because inputs from seven stakeholder teams landed late or out of order, and escalations had no single owner, so risk surfaced too late to act on.
approach: Mapped the renewal as a handoff chain and inserted coordination discipline at the seams — chasing inputs before they were late, surfacing blockers proactively, and making escalation ownership explicit instead of diffuse.
decision: Took the escalation point-of-contact role centrally rather than distributing it — concentration risk on me, but it removed the "thought someone else had it" failure mode that was costing the team deals.
solution: Lifecycle operations playbook for the portfolio: timeline ownership, proactive stakeholder chase, escalation triage as Lead Specialist, and mentorship so new teammates absorbed the coordination discipline faster (reduced onboarding time).
artifact:
artifact_alt:
outcome: Reduced coordination cycle time by two weeks across the portfolio; sustained 95% team success rate over five quarters as Lead Specialist.
reflection: Centralizing escalations on one person doesn't scale past that person — it worked because I owned it, and it would have broken the day I left without a documented handoff. The durable fix is the playbook, not the person; I'd build the bench earlier.
